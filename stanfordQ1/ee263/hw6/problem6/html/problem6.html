A

We can see that the eigenvectors corresponding to lambda 1 and lambda 2 are just complex conjugates of each other. We also know that the invariant plane is defined as the span(real(v), imag(v)). Therefore, the two orthonormal basis vectors are simply the real part and the imaginary part of the first two eigenvectors.

q1  = real(V(:,1));
q1 = q1/norm(q1);
q2  = imag(V(:,2));
q2 = q2/norm(q2);

C

x0 = q1;
x = zeros(4,40);
x(:,1) = x0;

alpha = dot(q1, V(:,1));
beta = dot(q1, V(:,2));

l1 = D(1,1);
l2 = D(2,2);
T = 0.1:0.1:40;
for i = 1:length(T)
    %We take a linear combination because q1 is just a linear combination
    %of the first and second eigenvectors.
    x(:,i) = real(alpha*exp(l1*T(i))*V(:,1) + beta*exp(l2*T(i))*V(:,2));
end

subplot(411); plot(x(1,:)');
subplot(412); plot(x(2,:)');
subplot(413); plot(x(3,:)');
subplot(414); plot(x(4,:)');
